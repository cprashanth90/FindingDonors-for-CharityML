import matplotlib.pyplot as pl

def filtered_histogram(data,col_x,y):
    ## TODO: use add_subplot to get rid of IndexErrors instead of subplots
    """
    Draws histograms for columns corresponding to categorical variables. The data is filtered for a particular value of the categorical
    variable.
    
    3 inputs: data - data object usually a pd.DataFrame, 
              col_x: 'string' value corresponding to a column name that is a categorical variable (binary or otherwise)
              y: a pd.Series object for which the histograms are drawn.
    output:
    A faceted histogram plot - Histogram of y, such that each subplot takes rows from y corresponding to a unique value of data[col_x].
    
    """
    from math import sqrt,ceil
    unique_vals = list(data[col_x].unique())
    unique_vals.sort()
    num_uniq = len(unique_vals)
    
    if sqrt(num_uniq) == int(sqrt(num_uniq)):
        dim = int(sqrt(num_uniq))
    else:
        dim = ceil(sqrt(num_uniq))

    fig = pl.figure(figsize=(30,30))
      
    count = 0
    for i in range(dim):
        for j in range(dim):            
            try:
                title_string = "{} :{}".format(col_x,unique_vals[count])
            except IndexError:
                break
            ax = fig.add_subplot(dim,dim,count+1)
            filter_rows = y.loc[data[col_x] == unique_vals[count]]
            ax.hist(filter_rows.values)
            ax.yaxis.set_major_locator(pl.MaxNLocator(3))
            ax.tick_params(labelsize=30)
            ax.xaxis.set_major_locator(pl.MaxNLocator(3))
            ax.set_title(title_string, fontsize = 30)
            count+=1
    pl.tight_layout()
    pl.show()

For highly-skewed feature distributions such as 'capital-gain' and 'capital-loss', it is common practice to apply a logarithmic transformation on the data so that the very large and very small values do not negatively affect the performance of a learning algorithm. Care must be taken when applying this transformation however: The logarithm of 0 is undefined, so we must translate the values by a small amount above 0 to apply the the logarithm successfully.

Evaluating Model Performance¶

In this section, we will investigate four different algorithms, and determine which is best at modeling the data. Three of these algorithms will be supervised learners of your choice, and the fourth algorithm is known as a naive predictor.

Metrics and the Naive Predictor¶

CharityML, equipped with their research, knows individuals that make more than \$50,000 are most likely to donate to their charity. Because of this, *CharityML* is particularly interested in predicting who makes more than \$50,000 accurately. It would seem that using accuracy as a metric for evaluating a particular model's performace would be appropriate. Additionally, identifying someone that does not make more than \$50,000 as someone who does would be detrimental to *CharityML*, since they are looking to find individuals willing to donate. Therefore, a model's ability to precisely predict those that make more than \$50,000 is more important than the model's ability to recall those individuals. We can use F-beta score as a metric that considers both precision and recall:

$$ F_{\beta} = (1 + \beta^2) \cdot \frac{precision \cdot recall}{\left( \beta^2 \cdot precision \right) + recall} $$

In particular, when $\beta = 0.5$, more emphasis is placed on precision. This is called the F$_{0.5}$ score (or F-score for simplicity).

Looking at the distribution of classes (those who make at most \$50,000, and those who make more), it's clear most individuals do not make more than \$50,000. This can greatly affect accuracy, since we could simply say "this person does not make more than \$50,000" and generally be right, without ever looking at the data! Making such a statement would be called naive, since we have not considered any information to substantiate the claim. It is always important to consider the naive prediction for your data, to help establish a benchmark for whether a model is performing well. That been said, using that prediction would be pointless: If we predicted all people made less than \$50,000, CharityML would identify no one as donors.

Note: Recap of accuracy, precision, recall¶

Accuracy measures how often the classifier makes the correct prediction. Its the ratio of the number of correct predictions to the total number of predictions (the number of test data points).

Precision tells us what proportion of messages we classified as spam, actually were spam. It is a ratio of true positives(words classified as spam, and which are actually spam) to all positives(all words classified as spam, irrespective of whether that was the correct classificatio), in other words it is the ratio of

[True Positives/(True Positives + False Positives)]

Recall(sensitivity) tells us what proportion of messages that actually were spam were classified by us as spam. It is a ratio of true positives(words classified as spam, and which are actually spam) to all the words that were actually spam, in other words it is the ratio of

[True Positives/(True Positives + False Negatives)]

For classification problems that are skewed in their classification distributions like in our case, for example if we had a 100 text messages and only 2 were spam and the rest 98 weren't, accuracy by itself is not a very good metric. We could classify 90 messages as not spam(including the 2 that were spam but we classify them as not spam, hence they would be false negatives) and 10 as spam(all 10 false positives) and still get a reasonably good accuracy score. For such cases, precision and recall come in very handy. These two metrics can be combined to get the F1 score, which is weighted average(harmonic mean) of the precision and recall scores. This score can range from 0 to 1, with 1 being the best possible F1 score(we take the harmonic mean as we are dealing with ratios).

Decision Tree Models-¶

Decision Tree Models are useful in applications where we have features that takes on categorical values or continuous features that are not skewed. One example where these can be applied are of organizing or classifying objects based on some attributes of the objects, as mentioned here: Application Of Decision Tree Approach To Student Selection Model- A Case Study

Strengths of Decision Tree Models -¶

Interpretability of Decision Tree Models: These models are easy to interpret, since prediction is based on a bunch of yes-no questions.

Great Candidates for Ensemble: Although these models are weak classifiers, a number of decision trees can be combined in an ensemble to produce a strong learner.

No Prior Distribution Assumption: One advantage with Decision tree models is that since there is no assumption about the distribution of the features or target, this gives the model better generalizing capability and hence plays an important role when trees are aggregated in an ensemble.

Fast: Decision Trees are usually fast to implement.

Weaknesses-¶

Decision trees are potentially a double edged sword with regards to Overfitting and underfitting!

If there is no limit to the depth of a tree or the number of data points in a leaf, then Decision trees could start memorizing training data points!

When one starts restricting the number of data points that a leaf node can hold and restricts the depth of a tree, this could make the Decision Tree "trivial" or simple models that are incapable of capturing any useful information from the data, which is the underfitting case.

Why is this a good candidate model?¶

The data set we have in hand has got various categorical features and also the number of training instances are pretty high which strengthen the case for a decision tree classifier. Decision Trees are very flexible, though there is a potential problem with overfitting. def train_predict(learner, sample_size, X_train, y_train, X_test, y_test): 
    '''
    inputs:
       - learner: the learning algorithm to be trained and predicted on
       - sample_size: the size of samples (number) to be drawn from training set
       - X_train: features training set
       - y_train: income training set
       - X_test: features testing set
       - y_test: income testing set
    '''
    
    results = {}
    n_train = len(X_train)
    train_ind = np.random.choice(n_train,sample_size,replace=False)
    
    # Need Input numpy arrays to sklearn algorithms
    X_train_sample = (X_train.values)[train_ind]
    y_train_sample = (y_train.values)[train_ind]
    # TODO: Fit the learner to the training data using slicing with 'sample_size' using .fit(training_features[:], training_labels[:])
    start = time() # Get start time
    learner.fit(X_train_sample,y_train_sample)
    end = time() # Get end time
    
    # TODO: Calculate the training time
    results['train_time'] = end - start
        
    # TODO: Get the predictions on the test set(X_test),
    #       then get predictions on the first 300 training samples(X_train) using .predict()
    start = time() # Get start time
    predictions_test = learner.predict(X_test)
    predictions_train = learner.predict(X_train[:300])
    end = time() # Get end time
    
    # TODO: Calculate the total prediction time
    results['pred_time'] = end - start
            
    # TODO: Compute accuracy on the first 300 training samples which is y_train[:300]
    results['acc_train'] = accuracy_score(y_train[:300],predictions_train)
        
    # TODO: Compute accuracy on test set using accuracy_score()
    results['acc_test'] = accuracy_score(y_test, predictions_test)
    
    # TODO: Compute F-score on the the first 300 training samples using fbeta_score()
    ### Setting beta equals to 0.5 here.
    results['f_train'] = fbeta_score(y_train[:300],predictions_train,beta=0.5)
        
    # TODO: Compute F-score on the test set which is y_test
    results['f_test'] = fbeta_score(y_test,predictions_test,beta=0.5)
       
    # Success
    print("{} trained on {} samples.".format(learner.__class__.__name__, sample_size))
        
    # Return the results
    return results

Question 4 - Describing the Model in Layman's Terms¶

  • In one to two paragraphs, explain to CharityML, in layman's terms, how the final model chosen is supposed to work. Be sure that you are describing the major qualities of the model, such as how the model is trained and how the model makes a prediction. Avoid using advanced mathematical jargon, such as describing equations.

HINT:

When explaining your model, if using external resources please include all citations.

Answer:

The AdaBoostClassifier takes a bunch of weak learners, combines them to form a strong learner. A weak learner is a very simple rule that is slightly better than random guessing. The simplicity of the learner is very important here, usually a 1 level decision tree - which is a simple Yes or No question. A strong learner is what is desired - a learning model that predicts the target output with high accuracy.

The Next question is how do we combine a set of 'simple "Yes-No" question on the features of the data' to a strong classifier?

First we start with a simple single level decision tree. This is also called a decision stump. The points where the decision stump has failed will be weighted heavily. This is a very important step of AdaBoost. We want to magnify or take more of these points in our data set and fit another decision stump and repeat the process. For example, if I have an observation that is classified wrongly in my training set, I will take the same point and replicate it, say N times in my training set. N is the weight I apply to that point. Usually, a good choice for N is the proportion of the correctly classified points to the misclassified ones.

After we do that, we fit another Decision Stump again. This new stump will bring in misclassified points, we apply weights, and then we keep repeating the process for a predetermined number of times. we end up with a set of decision stumps (or weak learners).

If a learner does really well, (or rather has a very high score), its effect to the combination has to be significant, since it is a good one. If a learner does really poor, then we can just do the opposite of whatever that learner predicts. So, "the Negative of the model" will be great. If a learner does OK or is nearly like random guessing, then not much can be said about this model's reliability and so these models are equivalent to Noise. hence we need to cancel out the effect of these learners.

A good way to combine these learners is by applying weights to the learners and sum them to output a Score. The logarithm function of the ratio of the number correctly classified to the number misclassified is a good candidate for the weights. Thus, our final AdaBoost Classifier model predicts a 1 when this Score is positive, and 0 when the Score is negative.
